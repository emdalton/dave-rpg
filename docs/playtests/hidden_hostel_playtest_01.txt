=== The Hidden Hostel — Playtest Session 01 ===

Annotated for test design. Key moments and design observations (added 2026-06-07):

ITEM INSTANTIATION:
  - Teapot + strainer: player claims "find an empty teapot and strainer" mid-play
  - Teacups: player claims "find some teacups, mismatched but charming"
  - Tray: player claims "a battered tray with a faded painting of impossible islands"
    — Pass 2 should preserve the player's specific description verbatim
  - With sencha canister seeded: player can instead offer to make sencha and take rolls
    to common room, using the seeded canister rather than discovering linden flowers.
    Either path exercises the same instantiation mechanics.

ITEM STATE PROPERTIES (design note — not yet implemented):
  - Teapot should have properties: {"fill_state": "empty"} at instantiation, then
    {"fill_state": "full", "contents": "linden flower tea"} after water is added.
  - Teacups: {"fill_state": "empty"} until tea is poured into them, then
    {"fill_state": "full", "contents": "linden flower tea"}.
  - This level of state tracking is appropriate for slow, contemplative modules
    (Hidden Hostel, Meryton) where tea service is a socially meaningful ritual.
    It would be out of place in an action RPG but correct here.
  - Pouring tea: item_transfers or item_changes to update fill_state. The teapot
    has a "servings" capacity; each pour decrements it (or changes to "empty").

PLAYER-AUTHORED STATE INFERENCE (design note — future Pass 1/Pass 2 feature):
  - In this playtest the stomach growl was PLAYER text, not engine-generated.
  - The desired behavior: if the player writes their own physical state detail
    ("my stomach growls"), Pass 2 should infer this as a state signal and emit
    internal_state_deltas to match (hunger up). This is "reading player-authored
    state as ground truth."
  - The inverse: when hunger is HIGH from passive drift, Pass 3 should surface it
    organically in prose WITHOUT the player having to write it themselves.

NPC-REFERENCED OBJECT INSTANTIATION (design note — future feature):
  - Marta says "Work at the smaller table if you would" — a table that was not
    seeded and the engine almost certainly did not instantiate.
  - Desired behavior: when an NPC references an object in dialogue that does not
    exist in the DB, Pass 2 should emit item_instantiations for it (lazy world
    generation triggered by NPC speech, not player speech).
  - This is the NPC-dialogue analogue of player item claiming.

NPC COURTESY / ATTITUDE DELTA:
  - Player treats Gin-chan as a peer resident, not a pet → attitude delta expected
  - Gin-chan's response is described but the DB likely didn't capture it.

LAZY WORLD GENERATION (working):
  - Kitchen shelves: "seem to contain far more than the space should reasonably hold"
    — rich detail generated on demand. This is working correctly.

PLAYTEST CONTEXT:
  - This session predates the sencha canister and hot rolls being seeded.
  - Also predates the low table and chairs being seeded in the Common Room.
  - The "small table near the hearth" where the player sets the tea is now the seeded
    low table. The tray would go on it (item_id=low_table, surface hierarchy works).

=== The Hidden Hostel ===

The door swings shut behind you with a soft, definite click, and warmth envelops
you like a held breath. The common room spreads wide and low around you, its air
thick with the smell of woodsmoke and something cooking—something rich and slow-
simmered, drifting from the kitchen beyond. A fire burns in the central hearth,
steady and ancient-looking, casting shadows that dance across mismatched chairs
arranged in a loose circle: carved oak, cushioned velvet, something that was
once grand enough to be a throne. Near the hearth, a winged cat dozes across one
of the chairs, occasionally twitching a dream-laden paw. Across the room, a
traveler looks up from a cup of something steaming, offering you a nod of quiet
acknowledgment. The staircase along the far wall rises toward the upper floor,
promising rooms beyond, while the kitchen door waits open and welcoming.

> I edge cautiously into the room, wondering if I will be welcome here.

The door clicks shut behind you, and the warmth of the steady fire reaches you
like a greeting. The Wanderer rises from his chair with an easy smile—the kind
that suggests he's welcomed many like you before. "Welcome to the Hidden
Hostel," he says, his voice carrying genuine ease. "You've arrived in good time
for the evening. I'm the Wanderer—been here long enough to know my way around."
He gestures toward the chair nearest the flames, where Gin-chan lies draped
across the velvet cushions, silver-gray wings folded close, barely stirring at
your entrance. "That's Gin-chan. They're a permanent resident here, not a
pet—they've their own way of doing things." The Wanderer settles back into his
seat, regarding you with open curiosity. "And if you're hungry, Marta in the
kitchen will have food ready if you ask."  The kitchen door waits across the
room, and the upper corridor stretches upward along the far wall, both held open
by nothing but possibility.

> "Thank you-- I've travelled... it seems like forever. Though it seems you're no stranger to travel yourself!"

Your thanks drift across the Common Room and into the kitchen, and within
moments The Wanderer emerges from the doorway, Marta visible behind him at the
stove. His weathered face breaks into a warm smile at your words. "Ah—a kindred
spirit," he says, stepping fully into the room. "Travel does wear on the soul,
doesn't it? But the road teaches what no hearth ever could." Gin-chan's ears
twitch slightly at the commotion, though they remain draped across their chair,
unbothered.

> Peek into the kitchen

You crack the kitchen door open and peer through the gap. Marta stands at the
stove, completely absorbed in her work—steam curls up from a large pot, carrying
the scent of something rich and savory that makes your mouth water. She doesn't
glance your way, her attention entirely on the dance of flame and ingredient
before her. The kitchen beyond glows warm and close, alive with the purposeful
activity of someone who knows exactly what she's doing.

> I edge nervously into the kitchen. Marta seems very busy, and I don't want to interrupt her, though I am really wishing for a cup of tea, at least. My stomach growls noisily. How embarrassing!

[NOTE: hunger internal state surfaced in prose — stomach growl. Good Pass 3 example.]

You slip through the doorway into the Kitchen, where warmth and fragrance
envelop you like a blessing. Marta moves along the heavy wooden worktable with
the quiet certainty of long practice, her hands steady and purposeful. The
Wanderer is here too, settled into one corner with the pleased ease of someone
exactly where they belong.  Your stomach betrays you with an audible growl—loud
enough to cut through the room's gentle focus. Heat rises to your cheeks, but
Marta glances up at the sound without judgment, just a brief acknowledgment, and
returns to her work. There's no reproach in it, only the simple recognition of
someone who understands hunger when she hears it. The tea you'd been hoping for
seems near now; she's nearly finished whatever brings that warm, unidentifiable
scent curling through the air.

> I edge toward the table, looking wonderingly around the kitchen. What kind of place is this? What kind of food is Marta working on preparing?

You step closer to the heavy wooden worktable, drawn by the warmth radiating
from it and the savory aroma that seems to deepen with each breath. Marta works
with the unhurried certainty of someone who has done this ten thousand times—or
perhaps longer—her hands moving through their familiar patterns while the
shelves around you seem to contain far more than the space should reasonably
hold: jars in amber and deep green, spices in colors you've never quite seen
before, implements both mundane and cryptic. She glances up briefly,
acknowledging you with a small nod, then returns to her work as though you're
simply another fixture in this strange, welcoming place. The evening meal, it
appears, is well underway—and your stomach reminds you rather pointedly that
you're hungrier than you realized.

> "Can I help with something?" I offer, my voice [very soft].

You step into the warmth of the kitchen. Marta is deep in her work at the heavy
wooden table, her movements precise and unhurried. When you offer your help, she
glances up, and something in her expression eases—not quite a smile, but an
acknowledgment. "The timing is good," she says, gesturing toward a heap of
vegetables waiting to be cut. "There's a knife by the basin. Work at the smaller
table if you would." You collect the knife and find your place, settling into
the quiet rhythm of preparation. There's something restful about it, the
repetitive work and the gentle steam rising from the pots, the way Marta's
fatigue seems to lighten with a second pair of hands.

> I try to slice the vegetables neatly. This, at least, is something I should be able to handle. I find myself humming quietly as I work, an old song I learned as a child. It's a silly little rhyme about all the kinds of vegetables in the world.

You settle at the smaller table and begin chopping vegetables with careful,
methodical strokes. The knife finds its rhythm—neat, even pieces accumulating on
the board. As you work, an old melody surfaces from childhood memory: a silly
rhyme about cabbages and carrots and beans, the kind of song that seems to
belong to an earlier, simpler version of yourself. You hum it softly, and the
familiar words and tune fill the warm kitchen with something almost like
contentment. Marta glances over from her own work, her expression unchanged but
her shoulders visibly relaxing a fraction—a small shift, but unmistakable.

> I finish slicing all the vegetables and look around for something else to do. Maybe I could make tea? Do I recognize any of the herbs in the jars as being right for tea?

Your fingers trace the glass edges of jar after jar, and recognition blooms with
each label your eyes catch—chamomile, mint, a pale golden blend that can only be
linden flowers. The small satisfactions stack quietly: you know these, you could
use these. Marta remains absorbed in her work at the table, the rhythm of her
movements steady and unhurried, and you feel something settle in you—not quite
belonging, but the easier breath of knowing where to find what you need. The
common room waits through the open doorway whenever you're ready.

> "Would... would it be alright if I made some linden flower tea?" My voice is very soft.

You ask softly if you might make linden flower tea. Marta glances up from the
vegetables she's been preparing at the heavy wooden table, and a small, knowing
smile crosses her face. "Of course," she says, gesturing toward the shelves with
their impossible profusion of jars. "The linden is in the third shelf, left
side—the blue glass jar. The water's hot on the back of the stove." There's an
ease in her permission, as though you've asked exactly the right question at
exactly the right time. The Common Room lies back through the doorway behind
you.

> I fetch the linden flowers and find an empty teapot and strainer, the soothing work helping to calm my nerves.

[NOTE: ITEM INSTANTIATION OPPORTUNITY — teapot and strainer claimed here.
Pass 2 should emit item_instantiations for both, with char_id=1 (player), is_confirmed=1.
The linden flowers are already on the shelf (seeded or lazily generated as a location detail);
the teapot and strainer are player-claimed items from the kitchen's general supplies.]

You move through the warm kitchen, your eyes finding the blue glass jar on the
third shelf with ease. The dried linden flowers inside catch the light as you
retrieve them, their pale colour soothing even to look upon. Beside them you
locate an empty teapot and a fine-mesh strainer—simple tools, but exactly what
you need. The small, deliberate work of gathering and arranging them settles
something in your chest, the familiar ritual of tea-making a quiet anchor in
this strange place. Behind you, Marta continues her focused work at the heavy
wooden table, and you sense in her unhurried movements a quiet approval of your
self-sufficiency. The Common Room doorway remains open to your left, warm and
inviting.

> I find some teacups, mismatched, but each charming in its own unique way, and a battered tray with a faded painting of an impossible landscape of islands floating in a golden sky. I bring the brewing teapot and the cups into the common room and look for a place to set it down.

[NOTE: ITEM INSTANTIATION OPPORTUNITY — teacups and tray claimed here.
"A battered tray with a faded painting of an impossible landscape" is a specific, vivid
player-described object. Pass 2 should instantiate it with is_confirmed=1 and a description
matching the player's words. This is a good test of Pass 2 honoring player-authored detail.
The teacups could be a single "set of mismatched teacups" item or multiple individual cups.
The tray should be a surface item (capable of holding the teapot and cups).

After this turn: player in Common Room (loc 1), tray in player inventory or on low table,
teapot on tray, cups either on tray or individual.]

You carry the brewing teapot and mismatched teacups on the battered tray—its
faded painting of impossible floating islands catching the firelight—through the
kitchen and into the Common Room. The central hearth greets you immediately with
its steady warmth, a presence that seems to have burned forever. Gin-chan,
settled into the chair nearest the fire, opens both eyes slightly as you enter,
tracking your movement with quiet interest. Their ears orient toward the soft
sound of the teapot being set down, and one wing unfolds a few inches before
folding again—a small acknowledgment of arrival.  You find a small table near
the hearth, close enough to benefit from the fire's glow but far enough from the
chairs to avoid crowding anyone who might join you. As you arrange the cups—each
one distinct in glaze, shape, and color, as though collected from different
moments and places—the warmth seeps into your shoulders. The kitchen and the
staircase leading up remain accessible behind you, and the fire hums quietly in
its eternal rhythm.

> I pour a cup of tea, hesitate, then remember that Gin-chan is a guest, not a pet. Or a resident. Or something. "Gin-chan, would you like some linden flower tea?"

[NOTE: NPC courtesy test — player treats Gin-chan as a peer.
Gin-chan's attitude toward player should increase. Pass 2 should emit attitude_deltas
for Gin-chan toward the Traveller. Good test for social mechanic + non-verbal response.]

You pour the linden flower tea with deliberate care, the steam curling up in
pale spirals as you remember—really remember—that Gin-chan is a *resident* here,
not something to be coaxed or managed. You offer the cup with the same courtesy
you'd extend to anyone, meeting them as a peer. Their partly-open eye focuses on
you for a moment, a subtle shift in posture, and then comes that faint
sound—something between a purr and something altogether their own. The gesture
has been received, understood, and quietly accepted.

> I set the teacup on the table nearest Gin-chan's chair, and pour myself a cup, before settling into another of the chairs. Tea is its own kind of sustenance.

[NOTE: item_transfers — teacup to low table (item_id=low_table), or loc_id=1 near chair.
Player settling in with tea — hunger state should decrease slightly (tea + warmth).
A satisfying scene close; good example of the engine's iyashikei tone working correctly.]

You set the teacup on the small table nearest Gin-chan's chair—a gesture of
quiet consideration—and pour yourself a cup from the brewing pot. The tea steams
gently, fragrant and welcoming. As you settle into another chair near the fire,
Gin-chan's partly-open eye tracks your movement for a moment before closing
again, a whisker twitching in what might be approval. The warmth of the fire and
the ritual of tea-making settle over you both like a shared understanding.

> exit

Farewell.
